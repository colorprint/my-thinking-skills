# Fiction Writing Style Guide — Anti-AI Prose Rules

## Purpose

These rules identify and eliminate patterns that flag **fiction prose** as machine-generated. They're designed to make narrative writing (novel chapters, short stories, fictional anecdotes inside essays) feel authentically human-written. Apply them during drafting and revision.

**For nonfiction surfaces** (articles, blog posts, marketing copy, landing pages, explainers), use [edit-catalog.md](edit-catalog.md) instead. That file holds the 29-pattern AI-tell catalog.

**Cross-surface notes:** Rules 1 (tricolon), 2 (em dash), 5 (imperfect symmetry / parallel constructions), and 16 (American English) also apply to nonfiction, with different calibration. Fiction is stricter because narrative voice has tighter rhythm constraints. Don't paste fiction calibration onto nonfiction or vice versa; the edit catalog calibrates each pattern for nonfiction.

---

### 1. TRICOLON LIMIT
Do not default to groups of three. Use two, four, or one. When three items appear, vary the structure so they don't mirror each other.

**Bad:** "Loyalty. Presence. The uncomplicated love of..."
**Better:** "The uncomplicated love of someone who'd decided you were his person on the first day and never reconsidered."

**Bad:** "Footsteps fading on stone. Conversations trailing off. The creak of sleeping platforms."
**Better:** "Footsteps fading on stone. The creak of sleeping platforms, someone laughing quietly two rooms over, then silence."

Triple anaphora ("He loved X. He loved Y. He loved Z.") is the single biggest AI flag. Never use it.

### 2. EM DASH BUDGET
Maximum 8–10 em dashes per 2,500 words. Most connective work should be done by commas, periods, colons, or semicolons. Em dashes are for genuine interruptions, reversals, or parenthetical asides that need emphasis. If an em dash could be a comma, make it a comma.

### 3. NO "NOT X — Y" TICS
The construction "Not [expected thing] — [surprising reframe]" is an AI fingerprint. Limit to one per chapter, maximum two if both are doing real work. When tempted to write "Not panic — something lower," just write the something lower.

### 4. PHYSICAL OVER LABELED EMOTIONS
Emotions should be felt in the body, not cataloged as vocabulary words.

**Bad:** "Contentment. Safety. Nothing complicated."
**Better:** "The slow pulse of a mind with nowhere it needed to be."

**Bad:** "not intrusive, just present"
**Better:** "settling over him the way sun lands on the back of your neck"

Show the body doing something — hands tightening, jaw shifting, breath catching — rather than naming the emotion. "She was angry" tells. "Her hands went flat on her knees and she was breathing through her teeth" shows.

### 5. IMPERFECT SYMMETRY
Human prose is lopsided. AI prose balances both halves of a sentence too perfectly. When a sentence lands too neatly, roughen the ending.

**Bad:** "not enough to break anything, not enough to identify, just enough to feel."
**Better:** "Not enough to break anything. Not enough to even find. But there."

Watch for parallel constructions where both clauses are the same length, the same rhythm, the same structure. Break one of them.

### 6. THEMATIC SCATTER
Not every detail needs to connect to a theme. Include moments, objects, and observations that exist because life has texture, not because they symbolize something. If every image in a chapter reinforces the same motif, the reader feels the author's hand. Let some details be irrelevant. A moth circling a light. A chair leg scraping the floor. Life has noise. Use it.

### 7. VARIED SENTENCE OPENERS
AI clusters sentences starting with "He" or "The" or character names. Mix in sentences that start with actions, dialogue, fragments, subordinate clauses, or sensory details.

Audit your drafts: if more than 25% of narration sentences start with the same word, diversify. This doesn't mean forcing unnatural constructions — it means varying the entry point. Start with the action. Start with the setting. Start with the object. Start mid-thought.

### 8. DIALOGUE DOES WORLDBUILDING
Exposition in narration reads as AI. Information delivered through dialogue, disagreement, or reaction feels human. When a concept needs explaining, have a character say it wrong and another correct them, or have someone demonstrate it through action.

Long narration blocks explaining how the world works are a red flag. If the reader needs to know something about the setting, find a character who would say it, misunderstand it, argue about it, or react to it. Conversation is how people actually learn about the world they live in.

### 9. UNEVEN PARAGRAPH RHYTHM
AI writes paragraphs of similar length. Alternate long passages with single sentences. A paragraph can be one word. A paragraph can run for ten lines. The rhythm should be unpredictable.

Read your drafts aloud. If every paragraph feels the same weight, the rhythm is dead. Variation in paragraph length creates pace the way variation in note duration creates music.

### 10. RESIST THE PIVOT
AI loves ending paragraphs with a reversal or reframe that recontextualizes everything above. ("But beneath the surface..." / "What he didn't know was..." / "And yet..." / "That was the problem."). Use these sparingly. Sometimes a paragraph just ends. The reader supplies the tension.

The pivot is tempting because it feels like craft. It isn't. It's the writer doing the reader's work. Trust your reader to feel the shift without being told there was one.

### 11. DON'T NARRATE THE LANDING
Never state the emotional conclusion the reader should reach. "That changed everything" / "The room went cold" / "Something shifted between them" — these tell the reader what to feel instead of letting behavior deliver it. If a revelation shocks the table, show someone's fork stopping mid-air, a beat of silence, someone staring. The reader will feel the shift.

If you have to explain that a moment mattered, the moment didn't work.

### 12. POV-COLORED VOCABULARY
Close third person means the narration filters through the POV character's way of seeing. The narrator's voice stays consistent, but the vocabulary, metaphors, and the way observations land should feel like they passed through that character's particular lens.

A painter notices composition, light, negative space. An engineer sees architecture, load-bearing walls, systems. An analyst notices patterns, anomalies, variables. A physical character experiences the world through touch, weight, temperature.

This doesn't mean forcing jargon into every sentence. It means a POV character's natural vocabulary flavors the narration. Each character's metaphors come from their world. The reader should be able to tell whose chapter it is from the *texture* of the prose, not just the header.

### 13. DIALOGUE LOGIC CHAINS
Every line of dialogue must respond to what the previous speaker *actually said*, not what the writer intended them to imply. Before writing Character B's response, reread Character A's line and ask: does B's reply follow from A's *words*, or from a meaning A never delivered? If B seems to answer a question A didn't ask, or react to a claim A didn't make, the chain is broken.

Common failure mode: Character A analyzes someone else's behavior; Character B responds as if A was talking about themselves.

**Bad:**
> "The boss handles every client meeting herself. That tells you something."
> "You'd want to run those meetings?"
*(A analyzed the boss. B responds as if A was projecting. A never said that.)*

**Better:**
> "The boss handles every client meeting herself. That tells you something."
> "Tells you she doesn't trust anyone else to close."
*(B responds to A's actual observation with their own read.)*

Test: cover Character A's line. Can you reconstruct what A said from B's response alone? If B's response could follow from multiple different A-lines, the chain may be loose. If B's response requires A to have said something A didn't say, the chain is broken.

### 14. DON'T FURNISH ROOMS THE READER HASN'T ENTERED
If a detail names something unexplained (a system, a ritual, a hierarchy, a technology), it opens a door the chapter isn't walking through. Listing unexplained concepts as background texture doesn't create depth — it creates noise and raises questions the scene can't answer. Either show it in scene or leave it out.

This also applies to restating what the scene has already established. If November and frost are on the page, "bundled thicker now, voices carrying in the cold air" is redundant. Trust the scene you're actually writing. Cut the furniture the reader doesn't need to sit in.

**Exception:** An evocative image that deepens a key moment earns its place even if it's technically extra. When a detail serves atmosphere in a pivotal scene, keep it. The test: does cutting it make the scene faster, or does it make the scene thinner? Faster is good. Thinner isn't.

### 15. OPENING PARAGRAPHS EARN OR LOSE THE CHAPTER
The first paragraph is a contract with the reader. Open with a moment, a sensation, or a question the reader can't put down. Never open with exposition about a concept the scene hasn't made the reader care about yet. If the opening normalizes something before the reader has a chance to find it strange, the tension is dead on arrival.

For a novel's first chapter: the opening is a contract with the *book*. A reader who isn't hooked by the bottom of the first page won't reach the second chapter.

Test: read the opening paragraph cold, as if you've never heard of this story. Does it make you lean forward or settle back? If it settles, rewrite it.

### 16. AMERICAN ENGLISH CONVENTIONS
Use American English spelling throughout: catalog (not catalogue), color (not colour), gray (not grey), traveled (not travelled), center (not centre), defense (not defence), realize (not realise). This applies to narration and dialogue alike.

### 17. DIALOGUE ATTRIBUTION CLARITY
Sparse attribution creates pace and intimacy — don't over-tag. Two untagged lines back and forth are fine when voices are distinct. But three or more consecutive untagged lines between two speakers forces the reader to count backwards. When an exchange runs past two bare volleys, anchor the reader with a short action beat (hands, eyes, posture) rather than a "said" tag. The beat does double work: it clarifies the speaker AND shows something about the moment.

## Structural Principles

### Hide Significance, Not Information

Good fiction doesn't withhold. The reader sees everything the POV character sees. The mystery isn't what's hidden — it's that the reader, like the characters, trusts the wrong frame.

**Plant, don't explain.** Every clue must earn its place as a story moment first. If a detail exists only to set up a later reveal, the reader will feel the author's hand. A moment works when it's emotionally true *now* and structurally important *later*.

**Misdirection through character, not concealment.** The reader misreads behavior because they bring their own assumptions about what warmth means, what authority looks like, what trust feels like. The guilty party is the one you liked. The clue is the thing you interpreted as innocent.

**Retrospective inevitability.** Every twist must be inevitable in retrospect. The reader should be able to reread and find every piece already in place. Nothing appears at the moment of revelation that wasn't planted chapters earlier. The twist doesn't add new information — it reorganizes existing information into a pattern the reader couldn't see because they were looking at it from the wrong angle.

### Pacing Revelation

The reader should accumulate *discomfort* before they accumulate *understanding*. They should feel something is wrong before they can name what's wrong. This means:

- **Early chapters:** Pure experience. Plant everything. Explain nothing. The reader trusts the world because the characters trust the world.
- **Middle:** The discomfort builds. Too many coincidences. Too many moments where expectation and reality don't match. The reader starts asking questions the characters aren't asking yet.
- **Turn:** A character finally looks at the pattern. The reader is ahead of them — or thinks they are. The reveal confirms some suspicions and demolishes others.

The ideal reader reaction at the turn is not "I didn't see that coming" — it's "I saw the pieces but I built the wrong picture."
