# The craft canon — deduped positive ruleset

The load-bearing prose rules from the major style authorities, deduped (shared rules credited to the originator plus key reinforcers, not stacked). The five-part core in SKILL.md is the spine; this is the complete set. Grouped by what each rule does.

## Clarity & concision
1. **Omit needless words.** Every word must do work. *(Strunk; Orwell, "if it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out"; Zinsser; even Pinker calls this "the most important maxim of good prose")*
2. **Characters as subjects, actions as verbs.** Readers feel prose is clear when the subjects name the cast and the verbs name what they do. *(Williams; the single strongest positive clarity engine)*
3. **Kill nominalizations.** Turn the abstract noun back into the verb it came from (*decision → decide*); nominalizations are "the primary cause of unclear, abstract prose." *(Williams; Lanham)*
4. **Active by default; passive on purpose.** Prefer active, but use the passive when the acted-upon is the reader's current focus. *(Strunk/Orwell for the default; Pinker for the deliberate exception, since the blanket ban is folklore)*
5. **Get to the main verb fast.** Avoid long wind-ups and long abstract subjects. *(Williams; Lanham, "start fast")*
6. **Keep related words together.** Word position shows relationship: bring related words and phrases together, keep unrelated ones apart, and don't split the subject from its main verb with a phrase that could move to the front. Misplaced modifiers breed ambiguity. *(Strunk & White, 4th-ed Rule 20; Williams)*
7. **Prefer the short, everyday word** to the long or jargon word. *(Orwell; Zinsser)*
8. **Positive over negative.** Say what is, not what isn't. *(Strunk; Williams)*
9. **Clear thinking is clear writing.** Murky prose means murky thought; ask "What am I trying to say? Have I said it?" *(Zinsser)*
10. **Beat the curse of knowledge.** You're too close to the subject to see what the reader doesn't know, and awareness alone won't fix it, so use a test reader. *(Pinker)*

### The Paramedic Method (Lanham): a mechanical concision pass for edit mode
1. Circle the prepositions. 2. Circle the "be" verbs. 3. Ask "Where's the action? Who's doing it?" 4. Put the doer in the subject, the action in a simple active verb. 5. Start fast; cut the wind-up. 6. Read aloud. (Optional: compute the "lard factor," words removed ÷ original; 50% is typical in bloated prose.)

### The cooling-off pass (Ogilvy's 1982 memo): edit-process, not text rules
Three habits that live *around* the writing rather than inside it, for anything that matters:
- **Sleep on it.** Don't send a letter or memo the day you write it; reread it aloud the next morning, *then* edit. The overnight gap is what lets you read your own words as a stranger. *(Ogilvy; reread-aloud reinforces #12)*
- **Get a colleague to improve it.** An independent reader catches what you're too close to see. *(Ogilvy; the curse-of-knowledge fix, #10, made procedural)*
- **Check your quotations,** and the facts, names, and numbers under them. Accuracy is part of craft; a wrong figure undoes good prose. *(Ogilvy)*

## Rhythm & sound
11. **Vary sentence length to make music.** Strings of equal-length sentences drone; so do strings of *loose* sentences (two coordinate clauses joined by and/but/so), which Strunk & White single out as the quickest route to monotony. *(Provost; Le Guin; Lanham; Strunk & White, 4th-ed Rule 18)*
12. **Read it aloud.** The ear catches what the eye skims. *(Lanham; Le Guin, "The Sound of Your Writing")*
13. **Right-branch.** Subject and verb early, modifiers trailing, so even a long sentence stays clear. *(Clark)*
14. **Emphatic word last.** Put the strong, new, heavy material at the end of the sentence. *(Strunk; Williams; Clark)*
15. **Cohesion: open old, close new.** Begin a sentence with familiar information, end with the new. This is what makes paragraphs *flow* between sentences, not just shine individually. *(Williams; the highest-value rule the anti-AI references lacked)*
16. **Express coordinate ideas in similar form.** Like content in like grammatical form; the likeness of form lets the reader *feel* the likeness of meaning (the Ten Commandments, the Beatitudes). Most load-bearing in lists, series, headings, goals, and tenets, where a broken parallel reads as a stumble. *(Strunk & White, 4th-ed Rule 19, parallel construction)*

## Concreteness & word choice
17. **Definite, specific, concrete.** Write with nouns and verbs. *(Strunk; Pinker endorses)*
18. **No stale figures of speech.** Avoid any metaphor or simile you're used to seeing in print; invent a fresh one or use none. *(Orwell)*
19. **Let the meaning choose the word**, not the word the meaning. *(Orwell)*

## Structure
20. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition.** One topic per paragraph; the opening of each paragraph signals a new step in the development of an exposition or argument. **Register tension with the core's story-first rule:** narrative *delays* the point (drop into a moment, land the lesson last); exposition/argument *fronts* it. Neither is wrong; the register decides. A memo's paragraph leads with its claim; a personal essay's paragraph leads with a moment. *(Strunk & White, 4th-ed Rule 13. The stronger "open every paragraph with a topic sentence" is Strunk's 1918 original Rule 9, which the 4th edition condensed into Rule 13, so prescribe the topic-sentence form by name, not as a 4th-ed rule.)*
21. **Start somewhere, go somewhere, sit down.** Beginning, middle, end. *(McPhee)*
22. **Structure from the material, kept invisible.** Let it arise from the content, "about as visible as someone's bones." *(McPhee)*
23. **Write the lead first.** It unlocks the structure of everything after. *(McPhee)*
24. **Chronology vs theme.** Expect the tension; chronology usually wins, but choose consciously. *(McPhee)*
25. **Crowd and leap** (Le Guin). Pack the sentence with what matters and is interconnected ("load every rift with ore"); leap over the rest, trusting white space.
26. **Gold coins plus a central question.** Reward the reader with a nugget every few hundred words; use internal cliffhangers; build the piece around one question. *(Clark)*
27. **Write in scenes, tell stories.** Narrative carries information faster than exposition; even abstract prose has characters and actions underneath. *(Williams; Clark; Elmore Leonard via Clark, "leave out the part readers skip")*

## Voice & stance
28. **Humanity.** Clarity, simplicity, brevity *and* warmth; "an aliveness that keeps the reader reading." Clarity alone isn't enough. *(Zinsser)*
29. **Classic style.** Prose is a window onto the world; show the reader what you see, as an equal in conversation. The default stance for essay, newsletter, blog. *(Pinker, after Thomas & Turner)*

> **Voice vs. "do not inject opinion" (the Strunk/White tension).** White's reminders say to place yourself in the background and keep gratuitous opinion out, which *looks* like it contradicts this skill's whole thesis (add voice, add opinions). It doesn't: White is warning against *padding* exposition with unearned editorializing, not against having a person on the page. The resolution is register. In **reportage and exposition**, keep opinion out unless it *is* the point; voice shows up as precision, rhythm, and stance, not assertions. In **essay, blog, newsletter, marketing, fiction**, voice and opinion *are* the product. Earned, relevant stance is voice; opinion tacked on to fill space is the thing White (and write-well) both cut.

### Voice discipline — the restraint half (E.B. White, *An Approach to Style*, 4th ed.)
Voice has a brake as well as a throttle. White's chapter adds no new *positive* rule the canon lacked. What it supplies is the brake, and it maps almost one-to-one onto this skill's **caricature** failure mode (voice overdone) and its AI-tell catalog, corroborated from the stylist's side:
- **Place yourself in the background.** Style emerges from substance; don't manufacture a "voice." Affect none and your own will show. *(guards against performed personality)*
- **Don't overwrite or overstate.** A single carefree superlative puts the reader "on guard" and taints everything around it. *(significance-inflation, named by a stylist)*
- **Don't affect a breezy manner.** Forced spontaneity reads as ego, not energy. *(the faux-casual tell)*
- **Prefer the standard to the offbeat.** Eccentricity for its own sake is the caricature cliff.
- **Don't explain too much.** Trust the reader; let dialogue and scene disclose manner instead of propping up "said" with adverbs ("he said consolingly") or explanatory verbs ("he consoled"). *(reinforces draft mode's "don't narrate the landing")*

## The meta-rule
30. **Rules are defaults with reasons, not commandments.** Keep the positive maxims; understand *why* a rule exists, then break it when it serves the reader (Orwell's own rule vi: "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous"). The grammar *superstitions* (never the passive, never split an infinitive, never open with "however," never end on a preposition) are folklore that often make prose worse (Pinker; Pullum). **Inviolable:** voice, specificity, clear thinking. **Calibration-dependent:** em-dash budget, tricolon limit, passive voice, sentence-fragment use. *(Strunk & White's Elementary Rules of Usage, 4th-ed Rules 1–11, the comma/colon/dash/agreement/pronoun-case mechanics, are correctness, not craft; write-well leaves them to a grammar checker and keeps to the composition principles, Rules 12–22.)*

> **Do NOT import** the grammar prohibitions as hard rules. Strunk and Orwell both used the passive in the very sentences banning it. Adding those superstitions would make write-well the rigid rule-bot its own thesis warns against.

## Genre re-weighting
Same rules, different ranking by surface:
- **Fiction.** Rhythm/sound, scene plus crowding-leaping, concrete sensory image, voice; structure invisible. Concision is subordinate to music; fragments and the passive are fair game for effect.
- **Essay / long-form.** Classic style, story-first lead into principle, cohesion, central question plus gold coins, warmth.
- **Marketing / copy.** Concrete specific outcome over vague benefit, emphatic word last, one central promise, tiny-story testimonials; kill hedging and promotional abstraction.
- **Technical / reference.** Williams' clarity (characters/actions, fast verb), Lanham's lard-cutting, curse-of-knowledge discipline. **Classic style does NOT apply:** a manual needs instructions, not a dialogue; the passive is fine when the system or result is the focus.
- **Newsletter / blog.** Classic style: conversational equal-to-equal voice, gold coins, internal cliffhangers, varied rhythm; looser structure than the essay, but the lead still does the work.
